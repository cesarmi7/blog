TOKYO!

Here, every corner tells a story, from the serene beauty of historic temples
to the high-tech frenzy of bustling districts like Shibuya

NRT to Tokyo!

Tokyo has two main airports, Narita and Haneda. I arrived at Narita Airport, which is approximately an hour away from the metropolitan area of Tokyo. After exiting immigration, on the ground floor, you'll find bus agencies where you can purchase tickets for destinations around Narita and the Tokyo metropolitan area. The bus ticket from Narita to Tokyo costs around 17 to 25 US dollars.

Additionally, if you go downstairs, you'll find the train station where you can take multiple trains. Don't worry about getting lost; you'll see a big sign saying "Train." Don't be afraid to ask for help if you need it. The most popular ones are the Narita Express or the Skyliner to Tokyo Central Station. In my case, with the JrPass , I chose to take the Narita Express.

Where to stay?

There are innumerable neighborhoods in Tokyo where you might want to stay. Where to stay can vary depending on your main interests, with options ranging from opulent hotels to reasonably priced hostels such as:


  • • Nui. Hostel & Bar Lounge
  • • Tokyo Guest House Itabashi-juku
  • • Citan Hostel
  • • Hotel Ryumeikan Tokyo

Personally, as a solo traveler, I prefer staying in hostels because I know that in such places, I'll always encounter people who may become friends for a lifetime. During my time in Tokyo, I made the decision to stay at Guesthouse Toco , a beautifully renovated guesthouse with a century-old history.

The moment one walks into this gorgeous place, a friendly bar invites guests and visitors to strike up conversations, fostering a cozy atmosphere. A friendly atmosphere made it easy to strike up interesting conversations with other travelers. The guesthouse draws a crowd of people from all over the world.

The unique space provides a genuine experience of staying in an old-fashioned Japanese house. The staff at Guesthouse Toco are very friendly, and the overall ambiance is pleasant. I personally enjoyed my time relaxing and chatting with other travelers in the charming garden. The location is convenient, just a few minutes' walk from the station and a short distance from Ueno and Akihabara.

Staying here was a wonderful experience as it was the first place I stayed in Japan, offering a peak of the beauty created by the contrast of temples, shrines, and homes side by side. The hostel also provides a breakfast option for 500 yen, approximately 3.35 US dollars. What can I say? It was delicious.

I know you're wondering, why are the chopsticks in the miso soup?! I don't know either; my brain wasn't thinking at that moment.

Edogawa!

I also had the opportunity to stay for about 3 days in Edogawa City because I wanted to experience life in a place not too far nor too close to the main metropolis. To be honest, I was very curious about the place with the same name like the famous author and critic Edogawa Ranpo , and one of my childhood heroes, Conan Edogawa. That, too, was one of the reasons why I decided to stay here.

I felt amazing staying around here, it's a peaceful place that gave me a lot of peace throughout the day, from the morning when kids leave for school until the evening when adults come home from work. Its understatedly beautiful streets constantly brought to mind TV shows like Detective Conan and Doraemon.

One of my favorite activities was having breakfast and dinner in the park in front of the apartment where I stayed. I always bought a salmon onigiri, another one with tuna and mayonnaise, and a coffee. I sat and appreciated the serenity of the place, listening to the birds in the morning, and in the evenings, the cats would come out to chat.

But undoubtedly, what surprised me was how children, young people, and adults of all ages used their bicycles to get to everywhere, the store, and the train station, where I assume they headed to the metropolitan area.

Without a doubt, Edogawa City is full of hidden gems and incredibly beautiful temples like Zenyoji and Zentokuji. Walking beside the Edo River is also a lovely experience. And if you happen to be here in early August, don't forget about the fireworks festival that happens every year on the first Saturday of August.

Asakusa!

Asakusa is a district located in the ward of Taito City. It is mostly known for its famous Buddhist temple, Senso-ji , which is the oldest temple in Tokyo. Asakusa itself is a very touristy and popular place due to its traditional shopping streets, such as Nakamise-dori, where you can find numerous souvenir shops and food stalls. That's why it's very common to see a lot of people in this area, but despite that, it is very colorful and has an incredible atmosphere. In this area, you will often see people wearing traditional clothing, and I love that some locals go there just to practice their English with foreigners.

Senso-ji and Asakusa, in general, are very close to Tokyo Skytree, which offers wonderful views of the city, but it has an entrance fee of 3,100 yen for people over 18 years old. An entirely free option is to go to the Asakusa Culture Tourist Information Center and take the elevator to the seventh or eighth floor, where the entrance is free, and you can take very nice photos of the area. But have in mind that visiting Tokyo Skytree is a wonderful experience in every sense.

If you are interested in immortalizing the charm of Asakusa without the overwhelming presence of crowds, I suggest exploring the area at night. The nighttime tranquility provides a unique perspective, with no more than 10 people scattered throughout the entire area, highlighting the immensity of the place.

Ueno!

Wandering through the streets of Tokyo, I arrived at one of my favorite places in this city, Ameya Yokocho, located on the border of the wards of Taito City and Ueno. Ameya Yokocho is an entire shopping street, and while shopping isn't one of my greatest hobbies, this place is inherently magical. It spans over a kilometer and is known for its vibrant atmosphere and diverse range of shops.

It's simply beautiful to see people who only come to do their weekly shopping, and many others trying the varied food. From 10 yen coin cheese to the rarest meats you've ever seen, you'll find all sorts of things.

You'll see all kinds of clothing, sneakers from all brands, clothes of different styles, and what caught my attention the most were the jackets with designs typical of the culture. This market is legitimate and lively, offering various goods, including fresh produce, seafood, clothing, accessories, and more. It's a popular destination for locals and tourists seeking affordable shopping and a taste of Tokyo's vibrant street market culture.

Ueno Park is a must-visit destination in Ueno. The park is incredibly spacious and beautiful, so expansive that you could easily spend your entire morning exploring its surroundings. Within the park, there's a Ueno Zoo where you can catch a glimpse of pandas. Unfortunately, when I visited on a Monday, it was closed. However, I still had a wonderful time exploring temples like Toshogu Shrine and capturing stunning photos of the park's scenic views.

What makes this park truly amazing is the presence of the Tokyo National Museum where you can find an extensive collection of art and artifacts of Japan's rich cultural heritage. In the park, you'll also see a pond adorned with beautiful lotus flowers that will captivate your senses where you can take a raft ride.

Shibuya!

I firmly believe that Shibuya City is Tokyo's most famous district worldwide, and it's quite evident when you're there. Best known for its incredible, busiest crossing globally, Shibuya Crossing sees about 1 million people pass through daily. If you wish to avoid the crowd, I recommend visiting before 9 a.m. or after 11 p.m., based on my experience when I went, things might have changed since then.

I highly recommend checking out Shibuya Sky, with an entry fee of 2500 yen, approximately to 16.80 USD for everyone over 18, which seems like a very reasonable price for the incredible views this place offers. In the same building but in different floors you can also find a lot of activities for you to have fun or relax.

Explore the district's diverse fashion landscape, from flagship stores to trendy boutiques on Takeshita Street, catering to the style-savvy crowd. Dive into Shibuya's entertainment scene with cinemas, theaters, live music venues, and a vibrant nightlife offering a plethora of options for a memorable evening out. And don't forget to visit the famous Hachiko statue right in front of the train station.

Beyond its modern facade, Shibuya reveals its cultural charm with the serene Meiji Shrine and a diverse culinary scene that ranges from traditional izakayas to innovative cafes. Shibuya seamlessly connects with neighboring districts like Harajuku and Omotesando, enhancing the allure of this Tokyo hotspot.

Shinjuku!

The bustling, lively streets of Shinjuku City are a testament to the city's never-sleeping image, offering a journey through contrasts and cultures seamlessly woven together. Nestled in the heart of this vibrant district is the infamous red-light district, controversial yet an integral part of Shinjuku's identity, showcasing a side of Tokyo that's both raw and real. Amidst these streets, the towering presence of the Godzilla statue stands as a beacon of imagination, symbolizing the indomitable spirit of Japanese cinema and pop culture.

The skyline of Shinjuku has been recently enhanced by the addition of Kabukicho Tower, a marvel of modern architecture that houses a diverse array of restaurants. This building alone caters to any culinary desire, from traditional Japanese fare to international dishes, all under one roof. The atmosphere within is imbued with a warm welcome, inviting you to indulge in the myriad flavors it offers.

A short stroll from the neon dazzle of Kabukicho, Omoide Yokocho, often referred to as "Memory Lane," offers a contrasting ambiance. This alleyway is a nostalgic journey back to a Tokyo of bygone days, with its narrow lanes filled with tiny eateries and izakayas providing an intimate dining experience. The air is rich with the aroma of grilled yakitori, and the sound of clinking glasses and shared stories fosters an atmosphere of camaraderie and warmth, reminiscent of the city's humble beginnings.

Shinjuku is more than its bustling streets and neon lights. It's a district where the past and the present merge, where the vibrancy of its nightlife is balanced by the tranquility of its hidden gems. The views from Shinjuku's skyscrapers at dusk, as the city begins to sparkle, are a testament to Tokyo's endless allure.

Shiba!

Shiba, a district in Minato City less trodden by the casual tourist, offers a contrast to the noisy streets of Shinjuku and the electric buzz of Akihabara. Here, one can find a harmonious blend of the old and new, where the serene Shiba Park provides a verdant respite from the city's relentless pace. As you walk through the park, the majestic sight of Zojoji Temple comes into view, standing as a testament to the district's historical depth. It's a place where you can indulge in quiet contemplation, away from the crowds, yet still feel the pulse of the city.

If you are as lucky as I was, then you might be able to see ceremonies at the temples. I would say that I was so lucky to be in a position to see and take a record of a certain ceremony known as Miyamairi. The rite enables the birth family to thank the deities for having enabled the child to be born safe and healthy. They, too, pray for the healthiness and happiness of the child in their lifetime. It's genuinely an interesting event to witness and to consider how this act marks the beginning of their spiritual journey in the Shinto faith.

The park is also a strategic vantage point for one of Tokyo's most iconic landmarks, Tokyo Tower. Its charming crimson and white colors attract visitors and offer panoramic views of the sprawling urban landscape, especially enchanting at dusk when the city lights start to twinkle. I fondly remember sitting and observing the scenery while eating a Tuna Mayonnaise Onigiri from 7-eleven and drinking a canned espresso.

Shiba truly is a mirror of Tokyo in every corner, a history-telling story, and every path telling of the fusion of Japan's storied past and its forward-thinking vision. One may admire some local architectural masterpieces, enjoy local cuisine in some little, yet authentic restaurant, or even be able to sit and watch local people just go about their daily lives. Shiba is a chapter in Tokyo's book that promises a more intimate experience with the city's soul.

Akihabara!

For all anime, retro, and Japanese pop culture fans! Akihabara is the district you absolutely cannot miss. As soon as you enter the area, you'll be greeted by giant signs featuring advertisements for various anime and nods to Japanese pop culture. The district is vibrant, with towering buildings and stalls filled with things to explore. Around the district, there are also shops selling crafts, and it's tax-free, so keep this in mind if you plan to make purchases.

And because I love this culture, I was on the hunt for a Nintendo 64 and a Nintendo GBA SP. I spent the entire day searching and checking out different stores to compare prices and differences between consoles. Just keep in mind that Akihabara is in the capital, and tends to have higher prices for these kinds of items. In other cities, I found figures and consoles at lower prices.

Of course, I couldn't resist trying my hand at claw machines! I tried them everywhere, big and small ones, and I ended up winning just a Sanji figure. So, I'm very happy with my achievement. You'll also find the famous maid cafes if that's something you're interested in. Personally, I skipped this as it's not my style of entertainment.

Chiyoda!

The Chiyoda City is something really beautiful and special. Walking through Hibiya Park, I am overcome by a very pleasant feeling of having the privilege of contemplating a true canvas of landscapes in front of me. One of my favorite parts was sitting down to watch the Crane Fountain located in one of the park's ponds. It was a scene of serene beauty in which not one but three found solace in the environment and captured the view on a canvas.

While peace reigned on this side of the park, on the other side the lively pulse of a fair vibrated. This was filled with live music and culinary tapestry from some sort of festival or event, the details of which now escape me. Maybe I didn't even know it or can't remember it. However, the joyful expressions and creative talent displayed are a feast of feelings to this day.

Right next to this park is the Imperial Palace. That's a view I really missed taking with my camera due to a bad angle. However, walking around is something I simply 100% suggest. Walking through its wide avenues just to taste what it has to offer is a truly inspiring experience.

Traveling to the Marunouchi district was, to say the least, an adventure. I highly recommend getting lost in the streets, visiting the shopping centers and feeling the sense of discovery that comes with it.

Ginza!

At night, Ginza in Chuo City, offers a different spectacle. The urban tapestry is illuminated with the greatness of modernity as its protagonist. The buildings, each planned with the glamor of lights and the precision of architectural mastery, stand as monoliths of progress.

And my special recommendation is to go to the Art Aquarium Museum. You could look at each aquarium and be amazed by its majestic simplicity. Without a doubt this type of art is wonderful and breathtakig that can leave anyone perplexed. Each tank contains a small world where goldfish dance in rhythmic synchrony. It is illuminated under a soft light, and these living sculptures swim in a kind of chaos that is charming at the same time, bringing peace to the soul.
